Forging Forward: A Capstone Master Plan for the Redevelopment of the Former US Steel Site in Duluth, MN

US Steel: Duluth Works Site History

The US Steel Duluth Works steel plant operated from 1915-1979 on the western edge of the city of Duluth, MN. The company executives originally believed Duluth's natural harbor was advantageously positioned at the interior of the Great Lakes chain near the booming iron ore and taconite mines of Northern MN. It was originally believed that the plant could produce steel cheaper than the establihsed smelting facilities in the east in steel centers such as Cleveland. However a global surplus of steel in later decades, primarily due to the dumping of cheaper steel produced from Chinese markets, led to the ultimate closure of the plant in 1985. Pictured here in a bhistoric image of the original plant is the material storage area, where large quantities of iron ore, coal, and other raw materials would rest before the steel prodcution process. Today, the only architectural relic that remains onsite in memory to former steel plant are the concrete pillings that sorround the material storage yard.

Recovery + Redevelopment

The US Steel site sized at roughly 640 acres is one of the largest US brownfield cleanups sites in the nation. An evironmental imapct study in addition to a Feasibility Study were completed in August of 2015. An estimated 700,000 cubic yards of contaiminated soils are scheduled to be excavated and caped in place in the upland areas of teh site over the course of the next three years. During this recovery effort, the southern approximately 120 acres of the site (included in the material storage area) is proposed to be repurposed into an idustrial heritage park and ecological and productive landsccape of nursery plants. Additionally, a cultural landscape libary to honor the first nation's historic wild rice harvesting grounds proposed for construction, along with a kayak boat house and hike in cabin sites located along an un-touched wooded pennisula. It is proposed that this portion of the site be turned over to a Public Land Trust in order to allow local residents connectivity to the site and once industrially restricted access to the St. Louis River Estuary that leads to the Duluth Harbor.
